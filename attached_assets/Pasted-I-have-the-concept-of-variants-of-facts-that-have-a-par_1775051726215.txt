I have the concept of variants of facts that have a parent-child relationship to the original. In my ranking system, should I treat variants as their own facts that can move up and down the leaderboard independently from their parent, or should I treat a vote for the variant as a vote for the parent and have the leaderboard only apply to the parents as a whole?

This is a fundamental data modeling question, and the right answer falls out of what you’re actually trying to optimize for with the leaderboard.
The core tension: A variant is semantically dependent on its parent (it’s the same joke, reworded) but experientially independent (a user encountering it may find it funnier or worse than the original).
Here’s how I’d reason through it:
If variants vote for the parent, you’re treating the leaderboard as a ranking of joke concepts. The parent becomes a container, and its score is the aggregate signal across all phrasings. This is clean and simple, but it creates a problem: a brilliant variant gets buried inside a mediocre parent, and a terrible variant inflates a good parent’s score. You lose signal, not gain it. Users also can’t express “this phrasing is better than that one,” which is arguably the whole point of variants existing.
If variants rank independently, you get a different problem: the leaderboard can get cluttered with near-duplicates occupying adjacent slots, which degrades the browsing experience. “Chuck Norris counted to infinity. Twice.” sitting at #3 and “Chuck Norris counted to infinity… twice.” at #7 is noise.
The first-principles answer is a hybrid: variants rank independently in the database, but you deduplicate at the presentation layer. The leaderboard shows the highest-ranked member of each fact family, with a “see N variants” expansion. This way:
	∙	Each variant earns its own score on its own merits
	∙	The best phrasing naturally rises to represent the family
	∙	The leaderboard stays clean
	∙	You preserve the full signal in your data
This also gives you a free feature: “community-refined facts,” where the original submission gets organically replaced by a better-worded variant over time. That’s a compelling engagement loop.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
